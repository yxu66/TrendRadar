The Fed’s dual mandate—low inflation and a healthy labor market—has created tension in decision-making. <br/>- <b>Core Inflation</b>: Predicted to rise to 3.1% by the end of 2025, with expectations for a gradual decline to 2.6% by the end of 2026. Inflation is not expected to return to the Fed’s 2% target until 2028. <br/><br/><b>Economic Context</b>: <br/>- <b>Growth Projections</b>: Officials forecast 1.6% economic growth for 2025, slightly higher than their June estimates. <br/>- <b>Tariff Impact</b>: Higher tariffs, largely paid by importers, are causing one-time price increases but have had slower-than-expected pass-through effects on inflation. <br/>- <b>Labor Force Constraints</b>: Trump’s immigration crackdown has reduced the supply of workers, contributing to the labor market slowdown. <br/><br/><b>Internal Divisions</b>: <br/>- <b>Dissenting Votes</b>: Miran’s vote for a half-point cut highlights divisions within the Fed. Seven of the 19 policymakers projected fewer rate cuts for 2025, suggesting future decisions may be contentious. <br/>- <b>Dot Plot</b>: Projections for 2025 varied widely, with one policymaker favoring rates to remain at 4.25%-4.5% and another suggesting a steep drop to 2.75%-3%. <br/><br/><b>Fed Chair Jerome Powell’s Remarks</b>: <br/>- <b>Risk Management</b>: Powell described the rate cut as a risk management move, aimed at supporting the economy rather than responding to an economic downturn. <br/>- <b>Focus on Labor Market</b>: Powell emphasized the Fed’s shift toward addressing labor market risks, noting that persistent inflation risks have declined. <br/>- <b>Independence</b>: Powell reiterated the Fed’s commitment to political independence, stating, “Everything we do is in service to our public mission.” <br/><br/><b>Future Rate Path</b>: <br/>- <b>Two More Cuts Expected</b>: Most officials predict two additional quarter-point cuts this year, bringing rates down to 3.5%-3.75%. <br/>- <b>Long-Term Outlook</b>: Rates are expected to decline slightly in 2026 and stabilize just above 3% through 2028.
